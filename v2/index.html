The vision

For decades, local leaders have envisioned a public pathway opening the Chewuch River shoreline to all. Now the Town of Winthrop and the Friends of the Riverwalk are making it real — a continuous, free trail that reconnects downtown to the rivers that made it.

The problem

One dangerous gap stands in the way.

For about 560 feet, anyone on foot is forced onto the Highway 20 fog line — families, elders, and kids, shoulder‑to‑shoulder with semis and logging trucks. The Riverwalk closes it.

The plan

Built in segments, from the bridge to the boardwalk.

  1. 01
    Funded · Building 2026

    The Chewuch Bridge underpass

    The first segment carries the route safely beneath a busy crossing — funded and slated for construction this year.

  2. 02
    Design underway

    Susie Stephens Trail → Confluence Park

    A 950‑foot path on micro piles that finally lets walkers bypass the Highway 20 fog line. $2M of $3.5M secured; easements donated.

  3. 03
    Along the way

    Bank & habitat restoration

    Repair failing bank stabilization where the Chewuch meets the Methow, and restore the shoreline for fish and wildlife.

  4. 04
    The vision

    One continuous Riverwalk

    Tie the segments together through Confluence Park and the boardwalk, with interpretive signs — a free path open to all.

Good to know

Questions, answered.

Is the Riverwalk really happening?

Yes. The first segment — an underpass of the Chewuch River Bridge — is funded and slated for construction in 2026, and a $2.06 million Washington State grant is already secured for the next segment. Landowners have donated the easements the route needs.

Where does my donation go?

100% goes to building the Riverwalk. Local donations are the match that unlocks the $2.06 million state grant, so every dollar you give brings state funding to the river. Gifts are received and managed by our fiscal sponsor, the Community Foundation of North Central Washington.

Is my gift tax‑deductible?

Gifts are managed by the Community Foundation of North Central Washington and may be tax‑deductible — please consult your tax advisor.

What exactly is being built?

A continuous, six‑foot‑wide, hard‑surfaced path on a micro‑pile support structure, built in segments along the Chewuch and Methow rivers. The project also replaces failing bank stabilization at the confluence and restores riparian habitat for native fish and wildlife.

How else can I help?

Give monthly, volunteer on a work day, or — if you own riverfront property — consider donating an easement. You can also simply spread the word. Email the team to get involved.
